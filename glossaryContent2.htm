TERMDEFINITION
ANHYDRITEAnhydrous calcium sulfate mineral. Gypsum without water of crystallization, and alters readily to gypsum. Slightly harder and less soluble than gypsum.
BEDA layer of sedimentary rocks or sediments bounded above and below by bedding surfaces.
BENTONITESoft clay or greasy claystone composed largely of smectite formed by the chemical alteration of glassy volcanic ash in contact with water. Bentonite can commonly absorb large quantities of water accompanied by a large increase in volume.
CARBONACEOUSRock or sediment rich in carbon or organic matter.
CHALKA soft, pure, earthy, fine-textured, usually white to light gray or buff marine limestone consisting almost wholly of calcite. It is porous, somewhat friable, and only slightly coherent.
CHERTHard, extremely dense or compact sedimentary rock consisting mainly of interlocking quartz crystals less than about 30 microns in diameter. It occurs mainly as nodes or concretions (chert nodules) in limestones and dolomites. Also known as flint.
CLASTICDescribes a rock or sediment composed mainly of broken fragments derived from preexisting rocks or minerals that have been transported some distance from their origin.
CLAYA naturally occurring material made up primarily of fine-grained minerals. As a particle-size term, generally considered to be particles below either 2 or 4 micrometers.
CLAYSTONEAny of a number of indurated rock types in which clay-sized particles are dominant.
COALA readily combustible rock containing more than 50% by weight and more than 70% by volume of carbonaceous material including inherent moisture, formed from compaction and induration of variously altered plant remains similar to those in peat.
CONCEALED CONTACTGeologic contact that is obscured by vegetation, soil cover, or other material and is located on the basis of interpolation from distant outcrops.
CONGLOMERATECoarse-grained clastic sedimentary rock, composed of rounded to subangular fragments larger than 2 mm in diameter (granules, pebbles, cobbles, boulders) typically containing fine-grained particles (sand, silt, clay) in the interstices, and commonly cemented by calcium carbonate, iron oxide, silica, or hardened clay.
CONTACTBoundary between two identifiable, adjacent masses of rock. A surface in 3-D space that intersects the map horizon (Earth's surface) to form a line on the map.
DOLOMITEA carbonate sedimentary rock of which more than 50% by weight or areal percentages is the mineral dolomite, or a limestone rich in magnesium carbonate; it is clearly associated and commonly interbedded with limestone, and usually represents a postdepositional replacement of limestone.
EOLIANPertaining to wind, especially said of deposits such as loess and dune sand, of sedimentary structures such as wind-formed ripple marks, or of erosion and deposition accomplished by the wind.
ESCARPMENTA relatively straight, clifflike face or slope of considerable linear extent, breaking the general continuity of the land by separating surfaces lying at different levels such as a plateau.
FISSILECapable of being easily split along closely spaced planes; exhibiting fissility.
FRIABLECrumbling naturally or easily broken, pulverized, or reduced to powder, such as a soft or poorly-cemented sandstone.
GLACIERLarge ice mass formed at least partly on land by the compaction and recrystallization of snow, moving slowly by gravitational creep, and surviving from year to year.
GRAVELAn unconsolidated, natural accumulation of typically rounded rock fragments resulting from erosion, consisting mainly of particles larger than sand (diameter greater than 2 mm), such as boulders, cobbles, pebbles, granules, or any combination; the unconsolidated equivalent of conglomerate.
GYPSUMMineral consisting of aquated calcium sulfate. It is the commonest sulfate mineral and can form thick, extensive beds interstratified with limestone, shale, and clay.
INDURATEDDescribing a rock or soil hardened or consolidated by pressure, cementation, or heat.
KAOLINITICMade up of kaolin clay minerals, which have lower base-exchange capabilities and absorb less water than other clays. Kaolinitic clays do not appreciable expand under increased water content.
KIMBERLITEFound in a small area in Riley and Marshall counties, kimberlites are igneous rock composed of at least 35% olivine, together with other minerals such as mica, serpentine, and calcite. They are forced to the surface through kimberlite 'pipes,' due to melting of olivine-rich peridotite in the Earth's mantle that is then forced upward due to lower temperature and pressure. Diamonds may be found in kimberlites, though there is no evidence of diamonds in Kansas kimberlites.
LAMPROITELamproites have been found in Kansas at two sites in Woodson County. Lamproites are similar to kimberlites, but have a different chemical composition. Like kimberlites, the rock itself is called peridotite, and is high in the minerals olivine and pyroxene. Though diamonds can also occur in lamproites, none have been found in Kansas.
LENTICULARResembling the cross section of a lens in shape. The term may be applied, for example, to a body of rock, a sedimentary structure, or a mineral habit.
LIGNITEBrownish-black coal intermediate in coalification between peat and subbituminous coal.
LIMESTONEA carbonate sedimentary rock containing more than 95% calcite and less than 5% dolomite. Formed by biotic or abiotic processes, are often highly fossiliferous, and can represent ancient shell banks or coral reefs.
MAP BOUNDARYCalculated line that bounds the mapping area.
MAPPING UNITVolume of rock or sediments defined by the distinctive and dominant, easily mapped and recognizable features that characterize it.
MARKER BEDA bed with distinctive lithologic features that is traceable over a wide area. Occurs within a mapping unit.
MICROMETERA unit of length equal to one one-millionth of a meter. One inch equals 25,400 micrometers.
MORTAR BEDDeposit consisting of sand or of a mix of clay, silt, sand, and gravel, firmly cemented by calcium carbonate and resembling hardened mortar. Also known as calcrete or caliche.
MUDSTONEAn indurated mud having the texture and composition of shale, but lacking its fine lamination or fissility; a block or massive fine-grained sedimentary rock in which the proportions of clay and silt are approximately equal; a nonfissile mud shale.
OBSERVED CONTACTGeologic contact that is located on the basis of direct outcrop observation and/or interpolation from nearby outcrops.
OOLITICPertaining to oolite, a sedimentary rock, usually a limestone, made up mainly of ooliths (a type of small, round or ovate accretionary body in a sedimentary rock, resembling fish roe and having diameters between 0.25 and 2 mm) cemented together.
OUTCROPThe part of a geologic formation or structure appearing at the surface of the Earth.
SANDDetrital rock fragment or mineral particles larger than a coarse silt grain and larger than a granule (62-2,000 micrometers), or a loose aggregate of unlithified mineral or rock particles of sand size; and unconsolidated or moderately consolidated sedimentary deposit of mainly medium-sized clastics.
SANDSTONEA medium-grained clastic sedimentary rock composed of abundant rounded or angular fragments of sand size with or without a fine-grained matrix of silt or clay, and more or less firmly united by a cementing material such as silica, iron oxide, or calcium carbonate; the consolidated equivalent of sand.
SELENITEThe clear, colorless variety of gypsum, occurring in distinct, transparent, monoclinic crystals or in large crystalline masses that easily cleave into broad folia.
SHALEA laminated, indurated rock containing greater than 67% clay-sized minerals; a claystone with fissility.
SIDERITEAn iron carbonate mineral. Usually yellow-brown, brown-red, or brown-black in color, though occasionally white or gray. It is often found as beds or nodules in clays and shales.
SILTRock fragment or detrital particle larger than coarse clay and smaller than very fine sand (4-62 micrometers), or a loose aggregate of unlithified mineral or rock particles of silt size. Silt commonly has high clay mineral content in the finer fractions and and high quartz mineral content in the coarser fractions.
SILTSTONEAn indurated silt with the texture and composition of shale but without its fine lamination or fissility; a massive mudstone in which silt predominates over clay; a nonfissile silt shale.
